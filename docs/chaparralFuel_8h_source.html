6/* ChaparralFuel
7 * Implements the Rothermel & Philpot (1973) "Predicting Changes in
8 * Chaparral Flammability" (Journal of Fotestry, Oct 1973) including
9 * modifications made by Jack Cohen in the FIRECAST program.
10 * Rothermel & Philpot are referred to as R&P in the remainder of this
11 * comment block.
12 *
13 * The purpose of the R&P paper was to assess seasonal and year-to-year
14 * changes in chaparral flammability. This was accomplished by providing
15 * chaparral fuel descriptors to the Rothermel fire spread model.
16 *
17 * The Rothermel fire spread model requires as input the (1) fuel bed depth,
18 * (2) fuel bed dead extinction moisture, and (3) fuel particle size, load,
19 * density, heat content, moisture content, and silica content by life
20 * category and size class.
21 *
22 * A major portion of the R&P paper is devoted to the derivation of the
23 * chaparral fuel descriptors over time (with the remainder devoted to the
24 * actual assessment of flammability over time). Towards this end, R&P:
25 * - applied a constant dead fuel extinction of 0.4 (Cohen uses 0.3);
26 * - applied constant fuel particle surface area-to-volume ratio, density,
27 * and silica content by life category and size class; and
28 * - applied constant dead fuel particle heat of combustion of 8000 btu/lb.
29 *
30 * The remaining parameters are either age-dependent (fuel depth and load)
31 * or season-dependent (live fuel moisture and live heat of combustion).
32 *
33 * First R&P developed a set of equations to apportion fuel load amongst the
34 * life and size classes based upon the total fuel load and a dead fuel fraction.
35 * They then provide a relationship between total fuel load and age,
36 * and between dead fuel fraction and age. Finally, they developed a relationship
37 * between fuel bed depth and age.
38 *
39 * R&P wanted to include seasonal effects on flammability, so they also
40 * developed a relationship between live fuel heat content and seasonal date,
41 * and between live fuel moisture content and seasonal date.
42 *
43 * We can summarize the capabilities of the R&P chaparral fuel models with
44 * respect to fire behavior modeling as follows:
45 * - fuel bed depth can be (1) specified or (2) estimated from age;
46 * - total fuel load can be (1) specified or (2) estimated from age;
47 * - dead fuel fraction can be (1) specified or (2) estimated from age;
48 * - live fuel heat content can be (1) specified or (2) estimated from seasonal date;
49 * - live fuel moisture content can be (1) specified or (2) estimated from seasonal date;
50 * - dead fuel moisture content must be specified;
51 * - all other fuel model parameters are constant over time, including:
52 * - dead fuel moisture of extinction
53 * - fuel particle surface area-to-volume ratio by life category and size class;
54 * - fuel particle density by life category and size class;
55 * - fuel particle total and effective silica content by life category and size class;
56 * - dead fuel particle heat content by size class;
57 */
